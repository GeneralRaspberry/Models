This post is an essay response to the question of "Biodiversity Champion Case Study & Action Plan"

Lytham Hall's National Collection of Sequoiadendron giganteum and Sequoia sempervirens demonstrate how historic estates can contribute to plant conservation, climate resilience research, and public engagement through structured horticultural management and volunteer-led stewardship. These conservation efforts are facilitated by indirectly by Plant Heritage, an organisation that officially protects nearly 100,000 different plant species. Subsequently, the proactive conservation efforts of The Lytham Hall team demonstrate the vital relational necessity between local action and global vision. Furthermore, with consideration for the changing climate, primarily caused by human activity: citizen action in alignment with the structure of local government and global strategic climate change policy is a priority. 

Though Redwoods are not native to the UK per se, they provide long-term climate and habitat benefits as temperatures shift in the North-West, with numerous benefit sui generis. For example, S. sempervirens is known to contain numerous tannins, giving the trees their reddish hue; which also contribute to their resilience to diseases such as fungi and insect infestations, and even contribute to their capability to survive wildfires. Thus these trees are an ideal conservation species, to provide numerous benefits with the changing pressures of climate change and globalisation. In addition, these tannins have been demonstrated to provide numerous benefits for people, thus enhancing their potential benefit as a biodiversity keystone species.

Data from the Met Office indicates that the Fylde Coast, the in situ location of Lytham Hall experiences average winter temperatures between 2.50-7.94°C between December and February, and annual precipitation of 886.41 mm per year. These conditions align with the native humid, temperate environments of Sequoia sempervirens and Sequoiadendron giganteum, supporting their long-term growth outside of their native ranges.

Under projections from the UK Climate Projections 2018 (UKCP18), the North West is expected to see warmer, wetter winters and relative stability in summer temperatures moving forward, enhancing the viability of climate-resilient trees as landscape anchors. This aligns with biodiversity goals under the Environment Act 2021 and Local Nature Recovery Strategies (LNRS). In particular, the LNRS for the Fylde Coast is maintained by the Lancashire County Council. LNRS were introduced under the 2021 Environment Act to drive nature's recovery and provide wider environmental improvements. Their main purpose is to identify and create or improve habitats most likely to provide the greatest benefit for nature and the wider environment.  In this way, the conservation activities of Lytham Hall with association with Plant Heritage and various other charities aligns closely with the local prerogative to establish habitats that promote the recovery of nature.

In addition, citizen science is a vital component of the LNRS; data provides a means to understand nature so that we can better facilitate it's recovery. By observing the growth rates of Redwood trees at Lytham Hall via volunteer records, the viability of Sequoia and Metasequoia species can be empirically quantified, and the data will support further interest in the establishment of Redwood trees outside of their native climate moving forward.
