This post is an essay response to the question of "Biodiversity Champion Case Study & Action Plan"

Lytham Hall’s National Collection of Sequoiadendron giganteum and Sequoia sempervirens demonstrates how historic estates can actively contribute to plant conservation, climate resilience research, and public engagement through structured horticultural management and volunteer-led stewardship. These efforts are supported indirectly by Plant Heritage, an organisation dedicated to safeguarding nearly 100,000 plant species. In this context, the work at Lytham Hall illustrates a crucial principle: effective environmental stewardship emerges through the alignment of local action with global ecological vision. As climate change—driven primarily by human activity—continues to reshape environmental conditions, coordinated citizen action operating alongside local governance and broader climate policy becomes increasingly essential.

Although redwoods are not native to the UK, they offer significant long-term ecological and climatic benefits as temperatures shift across the North West. Species such as Sequoia sempervirens possess high concentrations of tannins, which give the trees their characteristic reddish hue and contribute to resistance against fungal pathogens, insect damage, and even fire. These traits position redwoods as highly resilient conservation species, capable of supporting biodiversity under changing environmental pressures. Moreover, the biochemical properties of these tannins have demonstrated wider benefits, further strengthening the case for redwoods as potential keystone species within novel ecosystems.

Climatic data from the Met Office indicates that the Fylde Coast—the in situ location of Lytham Hall—experiences average winter temperatures ranging from 2.5°C to 7.9°C between December and February, alongside annual precipitation of approximately 886 mm. These conditions closely resemble the humid, temperate environments within the native ranges of both Sequoia sempervirens and Sequoiadendron giganteum, supporting their viability for long-term growth outside their original habitats.

Projections from the UK Climate Projections 2018 (UKCP18) suggest that the North West will experience warmer, wetter winters alongside relatively stable summer temperatures. Such trends further enhance the suitability of climate-resilient tree species as structural anchors within managed landscapes. This aligns with biodiversity objectives outlined in the Environment Act 2021 and Local Nature Recovery Strategies (LNRS), including those implemented by Lancashire County Council for the Fylde Coast. LNRS frameworks aim to identify, restore, and enhance habitats that deliver the greatest ecological benefit, reinforcing the role of sites such as Lytham Hall in contributing to regional nature recovery.

Citizen science forms a critical component of this process. Data collection enables a deeper understanding of ecological dynamics and informs adaptive management strategies. Through volunteer-led monitoring of redwood growth at Lytham Hall, the performance of Sequoia and Metasequoia species can be empirically assessed, providing valuable insight into their long-term viability under UK conditions and supporting future conservation initiatives.

This study is complemented by a working habitat model that simulates ecological interactions under varying structural and climatic conditions. The model incorporates key variables such as habitat suitability, seasonal flowering dynamics, and trophic relationships between producers, primary consumers (pollinators), secondary consumers (frogs), and tertiary consumers (birds). Keystone plant species are represented through temporally constrained flowering periods, reflecting real phenological patterns. For example:

Daisy (Bellis perennis): flowering between May and September, supporting pollinator populations during peak activity periods
Red Campion (Silene dioica): extended flowering from April to October, providing sustained nectar resources
Winter crops: active between November and February, offering limited but critical support during resource-scarce periods

In addition, tree presence introduces a microclimatic buffering effect within the model, reducing thermal stress on pollinators and influencing energy dynamics. This reflects the ecological role of canopy structures in moderating local environments.

Through the integration of empirical context and computational modelling, this work demonstrates how designed landscapes can function as adaptive ecological systems—supporting biodiversity, informing conservation strategy, and responding dynamically to the pressures of a changing climate.
